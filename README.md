# Introducing the Speaker
## Gayle Simpson Garrison
Gayle Simpson Garrison grew up as a Navy kid, moving around the country. She became interested in historyand political science while living in Northern Virginia. She spent her last two years of high school in Jacksonville before heading off to Samford University in Birmingham, Alabama where she majored in Political Science with a concentration in Public Administration.
After college, Gayle worked on Capitol Hill for Congressman Cliff Stearns. She married Sam Garrison in 1999, and in 2001 they moved back to Jacksonville after Sam finished law school. Gayle taught in the history department at St. Johns Country Day School in Orange Park for 16 years. During that time, she completed a Master of Arts Degree in American History from Gettysburg College through the Gilder Lehrman Institue for American History.
Her presentation for the Jacksonville History Center is based on her Master's Thesis, an oral history on the closure and redevelopment of Cecil and its impact on the City of Jacksonville.

# The History of Cecil Field
<p>
In the years leading up to World War II, the United States began expanding its military infrastructure. This shift was a result of president Franklin D. Roosevelt urging the perspective of the U.S.'s involvment as more active to act as the "Arsenal of Democracy". As tensions escalated and the U.S. entered the war following the attack on Pearl Harbor in 1941. Jacksonville’s west side was rapidly developed into a naval air facility shortly after the U.S. entered the war. Cecil field was officially commisioned on February 1943 and swiftly became a prominent training center for carrier pilots and aircrews for combat in the Atlatic and Pacific. After the war ended in 1945, Cecil field became deactivated and closed for a short amount of time. However, it reopened as the Cold War began to neccessitate military preperation. By 1952, it was renamed as the full Naval Air Station and became the largest military base in Jacksonville. The base was highly prevelant in different dangerous event in U.S. history like the Cuban Missle Crisis and the Vietnam War. Cecil Field diligently acted as a base of support, turning out some of the Navy's top piolets and participating in numerous military operations around the world. At this point, Cecil Field had bound itself to Jacksonville's identity as a Naval city. Despite Cecil Field's longstanding service, it was chosen for closure under the Base Realignment and Closure (BRAC) process after the end of the Cold War. Due to shifting military policies, after nearly 60 years of operation the base was officially closed on September 30, 1999.</p>
